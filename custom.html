Based on your experience level, here's the recommended structure. You'll adjust individual block lengths in the next steps.
Why these block lengths?

Block lengths aren't arbitrary — they reflect how long it takes your body to actually adapt at each stage. A novice can drive adaptation in 4 weeks because everything is new stimulus. An elite lifter needs 8–12 weeks in the hypertrophy block just to build enough new tissue to meaningfully move the needle.

The key rule: you must earn your way into heavier training. Skipping the hypertrophy block and jumping straight to peaking is like trying to build a skyscraper without a foundation. You'll hit your ceiling faster, and the ceiling will be lower.

How long should each block be?

The Rule of Minimum Effective Volume

A block must be long enough to drive a real adaptation — but short enough that you don't plateau before it ends. Too short and you never fully stress the system. Too long and you plateau, get bored, and risk injury.

Novice (0–1 Year)

4 weeks per block is usually enough. Your body responds so quickly to new stimulus that you don't need the extra volume a longer block provides.

Intermediate (1–3 Years)

The sweet spot is 4–6 weeks per block. This is the standard 12-week meet prep cycle you see in most popular powerlifting programs.

Experienced (3+ Years)

6–12 weeks in hypertrophy, 6–8 in strength. The heavier the weight you move, the harder it is to drive change. You need more time in each phase to accumulate enough volume to force an adaptation.

Frequency recommendations — why these numbers?

Bench Press: 3–4× per week (High Frequency)

The upper body recovers fast and the bench is a highly technical skill. More practice means a better groove. Even a massive 440 lb bench creates far less CNS fatigue than a heavy squat or pull, so you can train it often.

Squat: 2× per week (Moderate Frequency)

The squat damages too much tissue to train 3× per week at high intensity, but once a week leaves you feeling "rusty." Two days — one primary, one secondary variation — is the sweet spot for elite lifters.

Deadlift: 1× per week (Low Frequency)

Pulling near-maximal weight creates massive spinal loading and CNS drain. Many elite pullers only compete-style deadlift once every 7–14 days, using lighter variations (RDLs, block pulls) for the remaining volume.

OHP: 2× per week

Moderate — enough frequency to build the pressing pattern and shoulder health, not so much that it competes with your bench recovery.

Why deloading isn't optional

Fatigue Masks Fitness

Heavy training builds up a massive fatigue debt — in your muscles, connective tissue, and CNS. That fatigue is physically masking your actual strength. When you train hard for 4–6 weeks straight, you're not operating at your true capacity.

The Supercompensation Effect

A properly timed deload lets the fatigue clear while the adaptations you built remain. You come back the following week noticeably stronger than you were on your heaviest training day. This isn't theory — it's what every elite lifter experiences before a competition.

Recommendation

For most people: cut sets by 50%, keep weight the same or slightly lighter, and focus on quality movement. 5–7 days is enough. Don't go full couch mode — you want your nervous system primed, not cold.
